7. MARSOC Assessment & Selection (A&S)

Gate 2 · Phase I: 3 weeks, Camp Lejeune · Phase II: undisclosed location · Conducted 3x/year

Any active-duty Marine from any MOS can apply, but I need 3 years TIS and must enter ITC within 6 years TIS. I need a GT score of 105+, a PFT of 235+, a 1st Class CFT, a Secret clearance, and strong command endorsement. A&S Phase I tests physical fitness (run 260+ PFT, 300m swim in utilities, 11-minute water tread). Phase II is mental and physical — location and full content undisclosed. Making it through A&S earns me a seat in ITC. Not making it sends me back to my parent command.

Other paths I might consider (if Infantry isn't my only option)

These aren't my primary plan, but they're worth knowing if I choose a different branch or MOS and still want a path to special operations or elite service.

Army — 11X Infantry → Rangers → Special Forces
Army Infantry (11B/11C) leads to Ranger Assessment and Selection Program (RASP) for the 75th Ranger Regiment, or Special Forces Assessment and Selection (SFAS) for a Green Beret slot. Both are highly competitive. Rangers require about 1 year of military service minimum; SF typically 3+ years. Army also has a 3-year Ranger contract (Option 40) that guarantees a RASP attempt. SFAS is roughly 24 days; the full SF pipeline including the Qualification Course (Q-Course) is about 1.5–2 years.
Army — 18X Special Forces Direct Enlistment
The Army allows direct enlistment as an 18X "X-Ray" contract, which guarantees a shot at Airborne School and SFAS. If I pass, I go through the full Q-Course to become a Green Beret. High attrition. If I fail any gate, I revert to another 11-series MOS. This is a fast-track option for someone who knows they want SF immediately, though Marine Recon → MARSOC is roughly comparable in prestige and capability.
Navy — BUD/S → SEAL → DEVGRU
Navy SEALs go through Basic Underwater Demolition/SEAL training (BUD/S) — roughly 24+ weeks of the most famous military training in the world. Hell Week happens in Phase 1. After BUD/S, SQT (SEAL Qualification Training) is ~26 weeks. Then assignment to a SEAL Team. DEVGRU (SEAL Team Six) is a separate tier above standard SEAL Teams. The Navy also has Special Warfare Combatant-craft Crewmen (SWCC) for a related but different SOF role.
Air Force — CCT / PJ / SOWT (now Special Warfare)
Air Force Special Warfare includes Combat Controllers (CCT), Pararescue (PJ), Survival, Evasion, Resistance, and Escape Specialists (SERE), and Special Operations Weather Teams. All share a challenging pipeline. The Air Force Special Warfare pipeline is now consolidated under a single assessment pipeline before diverging into career tracks. These roles are smaller communities but highly skilled.
Marine Corps — Officer route to Recon/MARSOC
Marine officers can pursue a Ground Intelligence Officer (0203) or Infantry Officer (0302) commission, and attend Reconnaissance Officer screening. Officers interested in MARSOC attend A&S and ITC just like enlisted, earning MOS 0370 (Special Operations Officer). ROTC, Naval Academy, OCS, or ECP (for prior enlisted) are the commissioning paths. Officer pay starts significantly higher than enlisted — O-1 base pay is ~$4,150/month in 2026.

My Path to Recon — step by step (2026 pipeline)

The Marine Corps overhauled the Recon pipeline in 2026. Here is exactly what I will face and when.

Two ways in
HZ Contract (Recon Direct)
I enlist specifically for Recon (HZ contract). I am guaranteed a pipeline attempt. If I fail any gate, I am reclassified per needs of the Marine Corps. I will need a 5-year contract. I get fast-tracked through training vs. open volunteers.
UH Contract (Open Infantry) + Lateral Move
I enlist as 0311 infantry (UH contract). After proving myself, I volunteer for a lateral move to Recon. I am not prioritized at holding platoons. The screening still applies. If I fail, I remain 0311. More flexibility but longer route.

My Path to Marine Raiders (MARSOC)

Who can apply

Any active-duty Marine from any MOS — but my Recon background puts me in the strongest possible position. Key requirements:

✅ US citizen, active duty Marine
✅ Minimum 3 years TIS — no more than ~6 years TIS at the time I enter ITC
✅ GT score 105 or higher on the ASVAB
✅ PFT score of 235 or higher (run a 260+ to be competitive)
✅ 1st Class CFT score
✅ Pass MARSOC physical screening and swim assessment
✅ Secret security clearance (ability to obtain)
✅ No more than 2 NJPs total
✅ Strong command endorsement and moral character
✅ Medically qualified for arduous duty

The MARSOC pipeline — total commitment

FY26 HQMC MARSOC Screening Team (HMST) visits
MARSOC runs screening team visits to bases throughout the fiscal year. I should find when the HMST is visiting my installation and attend the brief even before I formally apply. This is where I learn the current standards, talk to Raider recruiters, and start the packet process. FY26 guidance was published September 2025.
A&S Phase I — 3 weeks, Camp Lejeune
Physical fitness events around the MARSOC PERRES program. I must score 235+ PFT on day one. Swim: 300m in utilities (no boots) using side or breast stroke, then 11-minute water tread in full utilities, then 4 minutes of blouse or trouser flotation. Phase I does not guarantee a Phase II seat — I am being assessed the entire time.
A&S Phase II — location undisclosed, ~3x per year
Mentally and physically challenging — both the content and location are not publicly disclosed. Marines who finish Phase I are not guaranteed a seat. Those selected move forward to reenlist for 6 years and receive orders to ITC.
Individual Training Course (ITC) / Marine Raider Course (MRC) — 9 months
Conducted at Camp Lejeune. Produces MARSOC Special Operations Officers (SOO, MOS 0370) and Critical Skills Operators (CSO, MOS 0372). Physically and mentally demanding throughout with significant drop rates for injury and performance even this late in the pipeline. Completing ITC earns me the Raider designation and MOS 0372.

Life forks: marriage, kids, housing, overseas, injury

These aren't interruptions to my career plan — they are part of my career plan. I need to think them through on purpose.

Marriage — the timing question

Option A: Stay single through first enlistment
Simplest option for chasing Recon and MARSOC. I live in barracks, save money, and stay mission-focused. High-tempo training and deployments are much easier without a spouse waiting at home. Downside: I may feel isolated; relationships started during this time face distance and op-tempo stress.
Option B: Get married after boot camp / early in first tour
I'm entitled to BAH (Basic Allowance for Housing) as a married junior enlisted Marine. This means I move off-base or into family housing. My monthly income effectively goes up with BAH added. But: I am now managing a household, a spouse's expectations and career, possibly kids, all while trying to pass Recon screening. This adds real weight. I should ask: does my partner understand the deployment and training tempo of Recon/MARSOC specifically?
Option C: Marry mid-career (after first enlistment)
I have proven myself, have rank and income, and I'm past the most uncertain gates. My spouse enters knowing my path, not hoping it'll calm down. This is often the most stable timing for both the relationship and my career trajectory.

Kids — the planning question

I should think about this before it happens, not after. Having kids while pursuing Recon or MARSOC creates a legitimate tension that isn't solved by ignoring it.

📋 TRICARE covers prenatal, delivery, and pediatric care — strong benefit
🏠 Family housing or BAH for off-base housing becomes available when I have dependents
✈️ Deployments with young kids: communication plans, family support network, and a spouse who can operate independently are non-negotiable
💰 Childcare on a junior enlisted salary is real money — plan for it before it happens
📚 MyCAA gives up to $4,000 in tuition assistance for eligible military spouses — a real benefit if my spouse wants school too

Housing — the financial decision

Barracks (single, junior enlisted)
Usually mandatory when single and junior enlisted. Essentially free housing — my biggest financial advantage early in my career. Every dollar I save here compounds. I should treat barracks as a savings accelerator, not a problem to escape ASAP.
On-base family housing
If I'm married with dependents, I may be offered on-base family housing. My BAH goes directly to pay for it. Shorter commute, base amenities, community support, and a built-in social network of other military families. The tradeoff is less privacy and less control over the home.
Off-base with BAH
BAH (Basic Allowance for Housing) is based on my rank, dependency status, and duty station's zip code. Living off-base gives me more freedom but comes with: commuting time, maintenance costs, utility bills, and the temptation to overspend on a nicer place than I need. I should ask: can I live off-base AND save meaningful money every month? If not, on-base is smarter.
VA Loan — buying a house
I am eligible for a VA home loan after 90 days active duty during wartime or 181 days during peacetime. No down payment required, no PMI, competitive rates. I can use it multiple times. The smart play: use it when I am confident I'll be in the same duty station for 3+ years, not at my first assignment. Moving and selling too soon can cost more than renting would have.

Living overseas

Whether it's MSG duty, Okinawa, Europe, or a MARSOC deployment abroad, overseas life has unique financial and family implications.

💰 Overseas COLA (Cost of Living Allowance) added to my pay in high-cost locations
🚗 Military car-buying programs available in some overseas locations (e.g., SOFA agreements in Japan, Germany)
🏫 If I have school-age kids, DoDEA (Department of Defense Education Activity) schools are available on most overseas installations
✈️ My spouse can travel with me, but some MSG tours restrict accompanying family — I need to check specific program rules
🌍 MSG watchstander tours: 3 tours × 12 months each in 3 different countries — single Marines only at watchstander level

Injury — the path I can't fully control

The Recon and MARSOC pipelines have the highest injury rates in the Marine Corps. I need a plan for this scenario — not because I plan to fail, but because having a plan means I won't panic if it happens.

Training injury during pipeline

Stress fractures, shoulder tears, knee damage, and swim-related injuries are common. Some Marines go through the pipeline multiple times. An injury may mean a medical hold and a re-attempt, not permanent elimination. The key question: did I maintain fitness, grades, and discipline leading up to it?

Combat injury or illness during deployment

If I'm medically evaluated and can return to duty, my career continues. If I'm medically separated, I receive disability pay based on rating, TRICARE, and veteran benefits. My savings and education progress at that point determine how hard the landing is.

My injury-proof floor

No matter what, by year 4 I want: at least 30 college credits done (via TA), 3–6 months of expenses saved, no car loan over my means, and TSP contributions started. If I get hurt at any point, these assets give me a runway instead of a cliff.

An E-5 with dependents at a high-cost duty station can easily see total monthly compensation of $6,000–$7,000+.

Special pays on top of base pay

🪂 Jump pay: ~$150/month (hazardous duty — airborne qualified)
🤿 Dive pay: ~$340/month (for qualified combatant divers)
⚡ Special Duty Assignment Pay (SDAP): Extra monthly pay for select billets including Recon and MARSOC positions
💣 Hostile Fire / Imminent Danger Pay: $225/month when deployed to hazard areas
🏕 Combat Zone Tax Exclusion: Pay earned while deployed to a designated combat zone is tax-free
💰 Reenlistment bonuses: Varies widely by MOS, grade, and service needs — can be significant for high-demand MOSs like 0372

My 20-year retirement math

📊 Legacy/High-3 system: 50% of average of highest 36 months base pay. Example: if I retire as an E-8 with 20 years (~$7,908/mo), pension ≈ $3,954/month.
📊 Blended Retirement System (BRS): If I joined after Jan 1, 2018, I'm under BRS. Pension is 40% of base pay at 20 years, but I also get TSP matching contributions (up to 5%) during service. Potentially better total compensation if I invest TSP contributions well.
🏥 TRICARE: Subsidized health coverage continues after retirement — a major benefit worth thousands per year in the civilian world.
🏠 VA loan: Still available after retirement. Can still buy a home with no down payment.
🎓 GI Bill: Can transfer remaining benefits to spouse or dependents after 6+ years of service.

School while in the military — my options and strategy

I don't have to choose between the military and a degree. I can build toward a degree throughout my career — slowly but steadily — so that by the time I separate or retire, I either have a degree or am close enough that the GI Bill can finish it.

Tuition Assistance (TA)

$4,500/yr · up to 100% of tuition

Available to all active-duty Marines. Up to $250/semester credit hour, $4,500/fiscal year. Max 2 courses at a time. Must apply through WebTA before the class starts with command approval. Cannot use during MOS school, PME courses, or SkillBridge. I keep my GI Bill intact for later.

Best tool while on active dutyGT 100+ to take 2 courses at once

Post-9/11 GI Bill (Chapter 33)

100% tuition after 36 months active duty

Covers full in-state tuition at public schools, fees, books up to ~$1,000/yr, and a housing allowance while in school. 36 months of benefits (max). After 6+ years of service, I can transfer benefits to a spouse or dependent child. Best used after I separate or retire for full-time school.

Housing allowance while in schoolTransferable to family after 6 yrs

Montgomery GI Bill (MGIB)

$100/mo for 12 mo = $1,200 contribution; up to 36 months benefits

I contribute $100/month for my first year of service (auto-deducted). Available after 24 months of active duty. Less generous than Post-9/11 GI Bill but can be used part-time during service for a small monthly stipend.

DANTES / DSST Exams

Free exam fees for active duty

DANTES Subject Standardized Tests let me test out of college-level courses for credit. A single exam can earn 3–6 college credits and takes one sitting. A great way to build credits during slow periods without time in a classroom.

Fast credit accumulationStudy independently, test once

CLEP Exams

Free for active duty under MyCLEP

College Level Examination Program. 34 subject areas. Pass a 90-minute exam, earn 3–12 credits. Free for active duty via the MyCLEP program. I can build a transcript significantly just from passing CLEP exams in subjects I already know.

Zero cost while active duty

Marine Corps University (MCU/CDET)

Free online PME with transferable credits

The College of Distance Education and Training (CDET) at MCU offers professional military education online. Some courses are transferable as college credits. Also counts toward promotion. Two birds, one stone.

Counts for promotionCan earn transferable credits

SNCO Degree Completion Program

Full-time school on active duty for SNCOs

Staff NCOs (E-6+) can be selected to attend full-time college while on active duty to complete a bachelor's degree — no leave required. This is a competitive program but a powerful option if I'm mid-career and haven't finished a degree.

Competitive selectionFull pay while in school

MyCAA (for my spouse)

Up to $4,000 in tuition assistance for spouse

If I get married, my spouse may qualify for My Career Advancement Account (MyCAA) — up to $4,000 toward an associate degree, certification, or licensure program. This means my household can have two educations building at once, both partly funded by the military.

✅ Definitely do this

Start TSP immediately. Take even one class per semester. Stay in barracks longer than feels comfortable — the savings are real. Pick mentors in your unit who are where you want to be. Ask hard questions before signing anything.

⚠️ Watch out for this

The E-4 car trap: getting your first Corporal paycheck and financing a truck. Marrying someone who doesn't understand military life. Burning bridges in your unit over small things. Skipping PT during training breaks. Letting pride stop you from asking for help after a failure.

💡 The thing nobody tells you

Failure at a gate is not the end — it is data. The Marines who keep showing up after a failure are often the ones who eventually make it. The pipeline weeds out people who quit, not just people who aren't physically perfect. Show up. Every time.
